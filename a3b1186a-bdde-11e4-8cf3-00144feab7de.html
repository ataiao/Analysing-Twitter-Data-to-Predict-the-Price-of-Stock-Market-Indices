Chart that Tells a Story UK

Chart that tells a story — the FTSE since 1999

The ups, but mostly downs, of UK blue-chips

Judith Evans

February 27, 2015
Jump to comments section Print this page

What does this show?

The FTSE 100 index topped its all-time high this week, meaning that if you bought a tracker fund in an income share class back in late 1999, your investment would now be worth about the same as it was at the start (though you would have been paid dividends of about 3 per cent each year).

Perhaps not quite time to crack open the champagne, then. But other UK indices present happier news.

As you can see from the chart, the FTSE 250 has had a much better run, returning 167 per cent — excluding dividends — over the same period. The FTSE Small Cap, meanwhile, grew 46 per cent.

What does that tell us?

Partly it’s about what was happening in 1999. Remember the dotcom boom?

“The FTSE 100 was hugely overvalued by the end of 1999 and therefore the peak back then was an anomaly,” says Adrian Lowcock, head of investing at Axa Wealth. He notes that expensive technology shares had entered the FTSE 100 index, displacing other companies.

We are in different territory now: the FTSE 100 is 50 per cent cheaper, judging by price-to-earnings ratios, than it was in 1999.

But investors should also bear in mind the unusual nature of the FTSE 100, which includes many international and overseas companies. This exposes the index to currency fluctuations and global trends such as the downturn in the mining sector. Only 23 per cent of revenues in the FTSE 100 come from the UK, says Anthony Willis, of the multi-manager team at F&C.

Video: The FTSE's new record

The FTSE 250, representing the 101st to 250th-biggest companies on the London Stock Exchange, is a different creature.

“It represents a diversified index which is much more exposed to the UK economy,” says Jason Butler, chartered financial planner at Bloomsbury Wealth.

This means the FTSE 250 has benefited more from the UK’s economic growth over the past few years.

What about the small-caps?

The Small Cap index has the potential to benefit from steep growth among companies, until they are promoted into the other indices. To some extent, this also applies to the FTSE 250.

“It’s easier to grow a company from a market cap of £1bn than one with a market cap of £20bn,” notes Mr Butler.

Smaller companies gain from being able to “utilise technology to exploit opportunities, and cut costs and adapt quickly,” says Mr Lowcock.

On the other hand, they are also more volatile and may go out of business, but smaller companies proved relatively resilient during the financial crisis.

Do any of these indices contain investment trusts?

The FTSE 250 and FTSE Small Cap both contain investment trusts, a type of fund that is listed on an exchange. The FTSE 100 contains only one trust, the real estate investment trust Land Securities.

Investors cut UK equities over election worries

A growing number of multi-asset fund and wealth managers are reducing holdings of UK equities amid concerns about the outcome of May’s general election. The FTSE 100 broke through to an all-time high this week, but some investors believe that uncertainty over the election result — together with worries over potential knock-on effects such as a referendum on UK membership of the European Union — could point to torrid times for sterling and UK stocks.

Trusts have become more popular over the past five years, leading to a narrowing of the average “discount” — the difference between the net asset value of the trust and of all its holdings.

These narrowing discounts have helped the FTSE 250 and Small Cap indices to rise.

So where to from here?

Mid-sized and small-cap companies currently look more expensive than large-caps, says Mr Lowcock. They “tend to do better in the early years of the economic cycle, which we have been in [for] the last three years or so,” he says.

Whether the FTSE 250 will continue to outperform is “anyone’s guess”, says Mr Butler, but “you can be sure that if a higher return is on offer, it comes with higher, but not always apparent, risks”.

When it comes to the FTSE 100, Laith Khalaf, senior analyst at Hargreaves Lansdown, says: “Investors shouldn’t get spooked by [the index] reaching new highs.” The relationship between stock prices and company earnings is far more important than this psychological threshold.
